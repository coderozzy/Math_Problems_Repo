    Solution — Task 1: Binomial Model (Quality Control)

    Problem Recap

    A factory produces screws. Each screw is either good or defective with probability \(p\) of being defective. We check 3 consecutive screws.

    1. Describe the random experiment

    The experiment: We inspect 3 screws one after another, and for each screw we record whether it is defective (D) or good (G).

    Why this is a Bernoulli (binomial) experiment:

    A Bernoulli experiment must satisfy four conditions — let's verify each:

    Condition Check
    Fixed number of trials \(n\) ✅ We have \(n = 3\) trials (3 screws inspected)
    Each trial has exactly 2 outcomes ✅ Each screw is either D (defective) or G (good)
    Trials are independent ✅ The quality of one screw does not affect others
    The probability \(p\) is constant across trials ✅ Each screw has the same probability \(p\) of being defective

    Key insight: The binomial model is the go-to model whenever we repeat the same yes/no experiment a fixed number of times under identical conditions.

    2. Determine the sample space \(\Omega\)

    Each trial has 2 outcomes (D or G), and we perform 3 trials. The sample space is the set of all possible ordered sequences of length 3:

    \[\Omega = \{D, G\}^3\]

    Writing them all out:

    \[\Omega = \{DDD,\ DDG,\ DGD,\ DGG,\ GDD,\ GDG,\ GGD,\ GGG\}\]

    Total number of outcomes: \(|\Omega| = 2^3 = 8\)

    Why \(2^3\)? By the multiplication principle, each of the 3 positions can be filled in 2 ways, giving \(2 \times 2 \times 2 = 8\) sequences.

    3. Specify the probabilities of the elements

    Since the trials are independent, the probability of any specific sequence is the product of individual probabilities.

    Let \(p\) = P(defective) and \(q = 1 - p\) = P(good).

    Outcome # Defective (\(k\)) Probability
    \(GGG\) 0 \(q \cdot q \cdot q = q^3\)
    \(DGG\) 1 \(p \cdot q \cdot q = pq^2\)
    \(GDG\) 1 \(q \cdot p \cdot q = pq^2\)
    \(GGD\) 1 \(q \cdot q \cdot p = pq^2\)
    \(DDG\) 2 \(p \cdot p \cdot q = p^2q\)
    \(DGD\) 2 \(p \cdot p \cdot q = p^2q\)
    \(GDD\) 2 \(q \cdot p \cdot p = p^2q\)
    \(DDD\) 3 \(p \cdot p \cdot p = p^3\)

    Verification — probabilities sum to 1:

    \[q^3 + 3pq^2 + 3p^2q + p^3 = (q + p)^3 = 1^3 = 1 \quad \checkmark\]

    Why multiply? Because the trials are independent, the joint probability of a sequence equals the product of the individual probabilities. This is the fundamental property of independence: \(P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B)\).

    The binomial formula groups outcomes by the number of defectives \(k\):

    \[P(X = k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k q^{n-k}\]

    where: - \(\binom{n}{k}\) counts how many sequences have exactly \(k\) defectives (the number of ways to choose which \(k\) of the \(n\) positions are D) - \(p^k\) is the probability contributed by the \(k\) defective screws - \(q^{n-k}\) is the probability contributed by the \((n-k)\) good screws

    4. Define what is considered a success

    In this model, a "success" is defined as finding a defective screw.

    Important note: The word "success" in probability does not mean something positive — it simply labels the outcome we are counting. Since we are interested in tracking defective screws, finding a defective is the "success" event with probability \(p\).
